## Active area versus strip length in the MINGO00 T_dif study
---

### Main conclusion
The relevant geometric quantity for the `T_dif` comparison is **not** the full strip
length of `300 mm`, but the **active area effectively seen by the detector response**.

The important conclusion of this discussion is:

- the strip itself is `300 mm` long
- the active area is smaller than that, because there is strip excess at the sides
- a direct manual measurement of the active area gave `276 mm`
- this is very close to the data-driven estimate obtained from the calibrated `T_dif`
  comparison between `MINGO00` and `MINGO01`

So the previous apparent "too long strip" problem in the simulation is better interpreted as:

> the simulation was effectively using the full strip length, while the real detector
> response is governed by a smaller active length, around `276 mm`

### Why this matters
In the comparison of calibrated `T_dif` distributions:

- `MINGO00` showed a wider `T_dif` span than `MINGO01`
- this suggested that the effective simulated X extent was too large
- the data-driven estimate pointed to an effective length around `270-272 mm`

That result is now physically explained by the direct measurement:

- **measured active area:** `276 mm`
- **full strip length:** `300 mm`

So the simulation should preserve the physical existence of the `300 mm` strip, but the
effective sensitive extent used for the X / `T_dif` relation should be associated with
the smaller active area.

### Practical interpretation
This resolves the apparent tension very well:

- the strip is indeed `300 mm`
- the detector does **not** use the whole strip as active sensitive length
- the measured active area of `276 mm` is close enough to the data-driven estimate that
  the remaining mismatch should be attributed mainly to timing smearing / reconstruction
  width, not to a large geometry mistake

So the current interpretation should be:

1. use the active area, not the full strip length, as the relevant X extent in the simulation
2. take `276 mm` as the best physically grounded reference value
3. treat the residual difference with real data as a timing-resolution issue to be refined separately

### Uncertainty from the T_dif edge width
The calibrated `T_dif` comparison was also used to estimate the effective edge width of
the distribution from Gaussian fits to the derivative extrema. This provides a practical
uncertainty estimate both in time and in reconstructed X.

Current median values from the comparison are:

- **real data (`MINGO01`)**
  - `sigma_T = 0.0765 ns`
  - `sigma_X = 15.29 mm`
- **original simulation (`MINGO00`)**
  - `sigma_T = 0.0457 ns`
  - `sigma_X = 9.13 mm`
- **rescaled simulation**
  - `sigma_T = 0.0419 ns`
  - `sigma_X = 8.38 mm`

This is important because it shows that the geometry issue and the timing-resolution issue
are not the same thing:

- the **active length** explains why the simulated `T_dif` span was too wide
- the **edge width** shows that the simulation is still too sharp compared with real data

So after setting the effective active length to about `276 mm`, the next refinement in the
simulation should be to increase the timing smearing until the real-data edge width is
reproduced more faithfully.
