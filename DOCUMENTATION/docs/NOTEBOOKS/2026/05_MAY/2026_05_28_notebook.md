# Efficiency Purity and Flux Recovery in a Four-Plane Telescope

## Framing the inverse problem

One conceptual clarification is essential at the outset. In particle and astroparticle analysis, the **fraction of the stream kept by a cut**,
\[
f_{\text{keep}}=\frac{N_{\text{pass}}}{N_{\text{stream}}},
\]
is not the same quantity as **purity**. Purity is usually the posterior probability that a selected event is genuine signal:
\[
\Pi_c=\frac{\pi_s\,\varepsilon_s(c)}{\pi_s\,\varepsilon_s(c)+\pi_b\,\varepsilon_b(c)},
\]
where \(\pi_s\) and \(\pi_b\) are the prior signal and background fractions in the original datastream, and \(\varepsilon_s(c)\) and \(\varepsilon_b(c)\) are the efficiencies of cut \(c\) for signal and background. This is why purity is not an intrinsic property of the cut alone. It depends on the composition of the original stream as well as on the cut response. A cut can have low keep fraction and high purity, or high keep fraction and modest purity, and optimality depends on the measurement goal rather than on any one scalar metric. citeturn17view0

This distinction matters immediately for multiplicity cuts such as requiring three-plane or four-plane coincidences. Those cuts are not merely efficiency cuts. They also suppress accidental or fortuitous coincidences. In the muography literature, Lesparre and collaborators show that accidental event rates fall steeply with coincidence order, with two-matrix and three-matrix accidental rates scaling as \(4N_1N_2\delta t\) and \(16N_1N_2N_3\delta t^2\), respectively. A stricter coincidence therefore usually improves purity, but it also changes the accepted phase space and the topology of the selected sample. citeturn25view1

Once cluster size, hit charge, track angle, noisy channels, zeroed channels, and trigger logic matter, the correct mathematical object is not a single efficiency number but a **detector response operator**. A standard detector-level formulation is
\[
\mu_i=\sum_j A_{ij}x_j+b_i,
\]
where \(x_j\) is the truth-level content in bin \(j\), \(\mu_i\) is the expected reconstructed content in bin \(i\), \(A_{ij}\) is the response matrix, and \(b_i\) is the background contribution. In the particle-physics unfolding literature, the matrix elements \(A_{ij}\) explicitly include migration between bins and inefficiencies, including events generated in a truth bin that are not reconstructed in any observed bin. That is the natural language for your problem because your cuts are acting on detector-level morphologies, not on pure truth labels. citeturn16view1turn5view5

A useful compact way to think about the time dependence is
\[
R_{\text{obs}}(t)=R_{\text{phys}}(t)\otimes A_{\text{eff}}(t)+B(t),
\]
where \(A_{\text{eff}}(t)\) absorbs geometry, detector efficiency, and selection effects, while \(R_{\text{phys}}(t)\) is the true physical flux or rate. Your data only observe their product plus background. Unless one of those factors is independently constrained, the other is not identifiable from rate data alone. That is the core inverse-problem structure underlying your concern. citeturn16view1turn10view0

## What the three-plane method actually measures

Your proposed plane-efficiency estimator is structurally a **conditional efficiency**. For plane 2, if one defines
\[
\hat{\varepsilon}_2=\frac{N_{1234}}{N_{1234}+N_{134}},
\]
then what is being estimated is not the unconditional intrinsic efficiency of plane 2, but the probability that plane 2 responds **given** that the event already satisfied the conditions defining the \(134\) probe sample. In probability language,
\[
\hat{\varepsilon}_2 \approx P(H_2 \mid H_1,H_3,H_4,\text{probe definition}).
\]
That logic is directly analogous to the tag-and-probe formalism used in ATLAS. There, the desired efficiency \(\varepsilon(\text{Type})=\varepsilon(\text{Type}\mid\text{ID})\,\varepsilon(\text{ID})\) cannot be measured directly, so \(\varepsilon(\text{ID})\) is replaced by \(\varepsilon(\text{ID}\mid\text{MS})\), explicitly introducing what ATLAS calls the “tag-and-probe approximation.” In other words, even at the LHC, the measured quantity is often conditional rather than absolute. citeturn5view2

There is also a very simple algebraic reason your corrected rate does not become the incoming rate. With your notation,
\[
\frac{N_{1234}}{\hat{\varepsilon}_2}=N_{1234}+N_{134}.
\]
By construction, that inversion returns the rate of events selected by the other three planes under the same probe definition. It does **not** return the incoming rate before those selections. The fact that you observed exactly this behavior is not a pathology of your implementation. It is a structural consequence of the conditional definition itself. ATLAS’s tag-and-probe formalism makes the same point in a different detector context: conditional efficiencies are extremely useful, but they are not identical to absolute acceptance corrections. citeturn5view2turn16view1

For the estimator to be meaningful, the denominator must also be defined carefully. In a robust telescope analysis, the denominator is not merely the raw count of 134 patterns. It should be the number of valid **probe tracks** built from the other planes whose extrapolated intercept lies in an active fiducial region of plane 2 and whose timing is compatible with a through-going particle. ATLAS requires explicit geometric matching between probe and reconstructed object, and CMS uses trigger selections designed to avoid biasing the efficiency under study. This is the same reason your probed plane should not participate in the trigger in a way that self-selects efficient events, unless that bias is explicitly modeled. citeturn5view2turn20view0

A particularly close analogue to your setup is the EEE cosmic-muon telescope framework. EEE uses the redundancy of three chambers to determine local efficiency maps for the third chamber. The chamber under test is ignored in the fit, a track from the other two chambers is projected onto the corresponding \((X,Y)\) bin, and the bin is classified as “detected” or “missing.” The tracking-efficiency map is then formed from detected and missing counts, and the final local-efficiency map combines tracking and counting efficiencies. The method is designed specifically for situations where no convenient in situ external calibration source is available and where local inefficiencies, dead strips, and time variation matter. citeturn11view1turn11view4turn12view3

That last point is important for your case. If your detector response depends on local position, angle, cluster size, or time, then a global plane efficiency is usually too coarse. The EEE collaboration explicitly uses local efficiency maps and then corrects data event by event with the corresponding local efficiency. This is much closer to what your telescope actually needs than a single scalar \(\varepsilon_2\). citeturn11view4turn12view3turn12view4

## Why the corrected rate is not the incoming flux

The deepest reason is that a scalar efficiency correction only inverts a scalar loss mechanism. Your telescope does not have a scalar response. It has angular acceptance, local dead regions, noise contamination, morphology-dependent selection, and cut-dependent background composition. The response matrix framework makes this explicit: \(A_{ij}\) contains both inefficiency and migration, and background enters separately through \(b_i\). Once this is true, the inverse problem is no longer solved by dividing by one number. citeturn16view1turn5view5

This is also why simple bin-by-bin correction factors are not regarded as a general solution in modern particle-physics analysis. Schmitt’s review of unfolding methods states that bin-by-bin corrections are often used because they are simple, but their results are significantly biased by the underlying Monte Carlo distributions; in the same review, the bin-by-bin method is explicitly described as disfavored and, in the summary, “should not be used” as a general unfolding strategy. That warning is directly relevant to the idea of correcting your selected telescope rate by a single efficiency proxy and treating the result as an estimate of the original flux. citeturn16view1

Large experiments deal with this by separating **fiducial** measurements from **total** extrapolations. ATLAS explicitly reports cross sections in fiducial regions reflecting detector acceptance and only then extrapolates to a total phase space using additional modeling assumptions. That practice is not stylistic. It is a response to the same identifiability issue you have encountered: detector-corrected counts inside a defined, validated fiducial region are defensible, while extrapolation to the fully incoming flux is necessarily more model-dependent. citeturn19view1turn18search1

Muon-telescope and muography work reaches the same conclusion from a different direction. In the Bayesian dual-inversion work of Lesparre, Gibert, and Marteau, the telescope acceptance and the integrated flux are described as “fundamental unknowns” that are inversely correlated. They therefore propose simultaneous inversion of flux and acceptance, and they use open-sky reference data to determine effective acceptance including channel defects. The practical lesson is that, without either a reference flux measurement or a trustworthy model prior, one cannot infer absolute flux normalization from selected counts alone. That conclusion is not a theorem specifically about your telescope, but it is the natural inference from the published inversion framework. citeturn10view0turn10view1turn25view1

A second subtlety is that “incoming flux” itself may be ambiguous unless you define the phase space. Is it the total integrated flux over all directions and energies above threshold, the flux through the active geometric aperture, or the flux of through-going events that would intersect all four planes? Those are different quantities. LHC analyses handle the analogous problem by defining fiducial truth selections to mirror experimentally accessible regions. A telescope analysis that aims at physical flux should do the same rather than using an undefined notion of the “original” rate. citeturn19view1turn16view1

## How large experiments treat efficiency and purity

The LHC answer to your question is not that ATLAS or CMS somehow know the absolute incoming truth directly from data. Rather, they decompose the problem. They measure conditional efficiencies on high-purity control samples, compare those to simulation, derive **data-to-simulation scale factors**, and apply those scale factors to the full simulated response used in analyses. ATLAS states this explicitly: the measured data efficiency and the efficiency measured with the same method in Monte Carlo are combined into an efficiency scale factor \(SF=\varepsilon_{\text{Data}}/\varepsilon_{\text{MC}}\), and possible biases from the tag-and-probe approximation tend to cancel in that ratio. CMS likewise measures trigger and identification efficiencies with tag-and-probe in \(J/\psi\) and \(Z\) samples and reports data/MC ratios by kinematic region. citeturn5view2turn20view0

Purity is then handled as a background-subtraction problem, usually with control distributions rather than with assumption-free counting. CMS subtracts residual background by a **simultaneous fit** to the invariant-mass spectra of passing and failing probes, using common signal shapes and background models, and it propagates uncertainty from both signal and background parameters. ATLAS similarly fits signal-plus-background models in pass/fail samples or sidebands to extract the signal yields needed for efficiency measurements. In practical terms, this is how “purity of a cut” is estimated in a serious analysis: not by intuition about the cut, but by a model constrained in a control observable. citeturn23view3turn23view4turn23view2

When detector effects are more complicated than a near-diagonal efficiency loss, experiments use unfolding or forward folding based on a response matrix. Three standard families remain central: iterative Bayesian unfolding following D’Agostini, SVD unfolding following Höcker and Kartvelishvili, and Tikhonov-regularized solutions such as TUnfold. RooUnfold exists precisely to compare such methods within a common framework and to propagate covariance matrices. The key point is not the brand name of the algorithm. It is that the experiment works with a **many-bin response operator**, not with a single correction factor. citeturn13search0turn14search0turn15search1turn1search3

A further lesson from large detectors is that one must separate **detector stabilization** from **physics-rate modulation**. For gaseous systems such as RPCs, CMS explicitly accounts for environmental pressure and temperature when defining the effective high voltage, and ATLAS operates RPCs with automatic high-voltage correction for temperature and pressure variations. At the same time, the physical atmospheric muon rate also changes with atmospheric conditions. Project GRAND reports that pressure measured at the detector is inversely correlated with muon flux, and Riádigos and collaborators measured an effective temperature coefficient \(\alpha_T=-0.279\pm0.051\%\!/\mathrm{K}\) for nearly vertical tracks in a ground-based tRPC detector. In a four-plane cosmic telescope, variations with pressure and temperature therefore have **two channels**: changed detector working point and changed physical muon flux. Those channels should not be folded into one empirical “efficiency correction.” citeturn21view1turn21view4turn22search3turn7search6

## A practical analysis strategy for your telescope

The most defensible first step is to define the quantity you want to publish. There are three qualitatively different goals: a corrected detector-level rate for a specific cut, a **fiducial flux** for a well-defined class of through-going events, or a **total incoming flux** integrated over a larger phase space. The first is straightforward. The second is realistic if simulation and control data are good. The third is only credible if you have either a calibrated open-sky reference or a physically justified prior on the incoming angular and energy spectrum. This is exactly the distinction encoded by fiducial-versus-total measurements at the LHC and by acceptance-versus-flux inversion in muography. citeturn19view1turn10view0turn10view1

The next step is to stabilize and map the detector response at the level it actually varies. If your planes are gaseous, the operating point should be corrected or monitored against pressure and temperature. After that, do not summarize the telescope with one plane efficiency per plane. Build **local conditional efficiencies** with a 3-of-4 projection method in bins of position, angle, time, and, if statistics permit, cluster size or charge class. Require explicit spatial and timing matching between the projected track and the tested plane, and exclude projected intersections outside active fiducial regions. This is much closer to the EEE strategy, to ATLAS probe matching, and to what your data morphology demands. citeturn21view4turn11view1turn11view4turn5view2

The simulation should then be upgraded from “flux generator plus geometry” to a **truth-labeled response model**. A useful template is the EEE framework, which implements GEANT4/GEMC geometry, detector materials, surrounding structures, a parametrized detector response based on measured cluster sizes and timing resolution, and explicit pseudo-trigger logic; it then validates both angular rates and integrated rates against data, achieving agreement at the few-percent level in the tested configurations. For your telescope this same philosophy should include dead or noisy channels, “offender” logic, zeroing procedure, trigger multiplicity, cluster merging or splitting, and any environmental dependence that affects detection probability. citeturn10view2turn12view1turn12view4turn26view0

Once that is in place, every important cut should be characterized by **two surfaces**, not one scalar: a signal efficiency surface \(\varepsilon_c(\mathbf{u})\) and a purity surface \(\Pi_c(\mathbf{u})\), where \(\mathbf{u}\) may include \((x,y,\theta,\phi,q,\text{cluster size},t)\). In Monte Carlo, truth labels provide these directly. In data, analogous quantities can be constrained through pass/fail fits or sideband/control regions just as in LHC tag-and-probe analyses. A charge cut, a four-plane requirement, and a zero-offender cut are different selection objects and should each have their own efficiency and contamination maps. Treating them as one “overall purity” will hide exactly the dependencies that control your bias. citeturn17view0turn23view3turn23view4

The mathematically correct path to flux recovery is then to construct a response matrix in the variables that matter physically. Truth bins might be binned in zenith angle, azimuth, multiplicity class, or deposited-charge class, while reconstructed bins contain the measured counterparts and explicit categories for “not reconstructed” and “fake/noise-selected.” Schmitt’s review is especially relevant here because it also warns that the most trivial closure test, namely unfolding data generated by exactly the same response and truth model used to build the response matrix, is not very meaningful. Independent Monte Carlo samples and altered truth models are needed to test bias. citeturn16view1turn15search1turn14search0turn1search3

A practical consequence follows. If no external absolute source exists, the quantity most worth quoting is usually a **fiducial flux or rate corrected for time-dependent detector response**, plus a documented purity for each cut, plus an explicit model-dependent extrapolation uncertainty if you decide to quote a total incoming flux. Taken together, the LHC control-sample literature and the telescope-acceptance inversion literature strongly support that approach. Your conclusion that “no efficiency correction will simply give the real incoming rate” is therefore essentially correct. What you *can* do is move from a crude scalar proxy to a calibrated fiducial measurement with controlled model dependence. citeturn5view2turn19view1turn10view0turn10view1

## Questions and bibliography

The following questions determine whether your inverse problem is identifiable and whether a given cut can be defended quantitatively.

1. What is the exact **signal definition** at truth level: all through-going charged particles, only atmospheric muons, only single-track events, or only events with a specific cluster morphology?

2. Which observables most strongly control detector response: transverse position, zenith angle, azimuth, cluster size, total charge, timing residual, or offender count?

3. Is the probe sample for plane-efficiency measurement **unbiased** with respect to the tested plane, or does trigger logic partly self-select efficient events?

4. Are accidental hits and noisy channels independent across planes, or are there correlated noise bursts that invalidate simple coincidence assumptions?

5. Does the 3-of-4 efficiency vary so strongly across \((x,y,\theta,\phi,t)\) that a global plane efficiency is meaningless?

6. Can you acquire or identify an **open-sky reference sample** under stable conditions to constrain effective acceptance experimentally?

7. Which environmental correlations are instrumental and which are physical flux changes? This should be tested explicitly, especially if the detector is gaseous. citeturn21view4turn22search3turn7search6

8. For each cut, what is the fitted or simulated contamination from noise, accidentals, misreconstructed tracks, and multi-particle events, and what control region constrains it? citeturn23view3turn23view4

9. Does the response model include surrounding material and local shielding well enough that the angular rate distribution is reproduced in data?

10. Do corrected or unfolded results pass **closure tests on independent simulation** and stress tests with alternative truth models, not only trivial self-closure? citeturn16view1turn12view4
